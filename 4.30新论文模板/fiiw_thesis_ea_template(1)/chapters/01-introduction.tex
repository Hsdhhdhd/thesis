\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Sedentary behaviour is commonly understood as waking behaviour with low energy expenditure while sitting, reclining, or lying \cite{sbrn2017terminology}. Prolonged sitting is a narrower, time-focused form of sedentary behaviour: instead of describing all low-energy waking behaviour, it concerns extended seated bouts. Such sitting has become a normal part of desk work, study, home routines, and transport-related daily life, with office-based workers often accumulating substantial sitting time during the working day \cite{hadgraft2015excessive}. This makes prolonged sitting relevant not only as a clinical or laboratory topic, but also as an everyday sensing problem: people may remain seated for long periods while working, travelling, or using screen-based systems.

The public-health motivation for monitoring prolonged sitting is well established, although the evidence should be interpreted as risk association rather than as proof that any single reminder strategy can by itself improve health. Sedentary behaviour has been linked with cardiometabolic and cardiovascular risk in public-health reviews and guideline updates \cite{katzmarzyk2019sedentary}. Occupational sitting has also been studied as a workplace exposure that may contribute to cardiometabolic risk profiles \cite{reichel2022occupational}. Together, these findings justify measurement and intervention research. They also make it necessary to define precisely which aspect of sitting behaviour is being monitored.

\section{From Sitting Duration to Pelvic Movement}

Many sedentary-behaviour measurements focus on how long a person sits or how often sitting is interrupted. This is important because breaks in sedentary time have been associated with more favourable metabolic markers in observational evidence \cite{healy2008breaks}. Experimental evidence and later reviews also support the relevance of interrupting prolonged sitting \cite{buffey2022_interrupting_sitting_meta}. However, total sedentary duration and simple break counts do not fully describe what happens inside a prolonged sitting bout. A person can remain seated for the same duration while making frequent lower-trunk adjustments, or can sit in a much more static pattern.

For this thesis, the relevant movement is not whole-body physical activity and not full clinical posture diagnosis. The technical target is pelvis-region movement during seated bouts, especially the distinction between static sitting and instructed pelvic rotation. This target is motivated by the biomechanical relevance of sitting posture and lower-trunk movement. Sitting posture can change joint loading at the lumbosacral region \cite{kwon2018sitting}. Postural shifts during prolonged sitting have also been associated with perceived musculoskeletal discomfort \cite{waongenngarm2020_postural_shifts_discomfort}, and dynamic sitting can alter lumbar kinematics during computer-work tasks \cite{davidson2024_dynamic_sitting_lumbar}. The aim is therefore not to validate clinical pelvic angles, but to determine whether a sacrum-mounted wearable can detect a useful movement class and support a local reminder pipeline.

\section{Monitoring, Feedback, and Integration Gap}

Objective sedentary-behaviour monitoring has a long history, including methods for quantifying inactivity and sedentary time \cite{chastin2010objective_sedentary}. Accelerometer-based sedentary measurement has been reviewed as one common objective route \cite{byrom2016objective_sedentary_accelerometers}, and posture monitors such as activPAL have been validated for everyday posture and motion measurement \cite{grant2006activpal_validation}. These systems help answer questions about sedentary time, posture category, and transitions between sedentary and upright behaviour. Wearable inertial sensing has also been reviewed for spine movement assessment \cite{papi2017wearable}. More directly for this thesis, recent work has evaluated IMU-based pelvic-rotation detection and sensor placement for this specific movement class \cite{liao2026imu_pelvic_rotation}. Together, this literature supports the use of body-worn inertial sensing, but it leaves a narrower engineering question: whether pelvis-region sensing, labelled real-world collection, compact local detection, and haptic cue delivery can be connected in one low-cost prototype.

Monitoring alone is not sufficient when the intended use is to prompt a change in behaviour or posture. Technology-enhanced sedentary-behaviour interventions have used computer, mobile, and wearable prompts to reduce sedentary behaviour \cite{stephenson2017technology_sedentary_meta}. Just-in-time adaptive intervention research has also formalised the idea of delivering support at appropriate moments based on changing user context \cite{nahumshani2018jitai_design}. Devices that measure and provide feedback on sedentary behaviour demonstrate the practical relevance of linking measurement to a user-facing response \cite{gill2018sitfit_feedback_validation}. In many cases, however, the feedback logic is based on a schedule, general activity context, or off-device processing rather than a pelvis-region movement detection outcome computed on the wearable itself.

Closed-loop wearable feedback is therefore the integration point for this thesis. Real-time vibrotactile feedback has been reported in posture-related wearable systems \cite{gopalai2011vibrotactile_posture_system}. Broader reviews also show that wearable feedback for posture and spine-control contexts is an active but heterogeneous research area \cite{battis2024wearable}. At the same time, embedded machine learning on microcontroller-class wearable systems must respect latency, memory, and firmware constraints \cite{diab2022embedded}. The gap addressed here is not the absence of any monitoring or feedback system. Rather, it is the specific combination of a sacrum-mounted IMU prototype, Android and PC data acquisition, labelled everyday seated-context data, Shimmer comparison, compact on-device classification, and a local haptic reminder for prolonged static sitting.

\section{Research Question and Objectives}

This thesis asks the following research question:

\begin{quote}
How feasible is it to develop and evaluate a closed-loop, sacrum-mounted wearable system that monitors pelvic-region movement and provides real-time haptic feedback based on detection outcomes during prolonged sitting?
\end{quote}

To answer this question, the thesis is organised around four objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item To design and implement a closed-loop sacrum-mounted wearable system that enables on-device prolonged sitting monitoring and intervention.
    \item To evaluate whether the prototype can distinguish static sitting from pelvic rotation during prolonged sitting across different participants and seated environments.
    \item To evaluate the comparability of the prototype IMU signals with a research-grade Shimmer IMU during prolonged sitting bouts.
    \item To evaluate whether real-time detection and haptic feedback can be executed within the memory and timing constraints of the device.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}

The first contribution is a low-cost wearable research prototype that integrates IMU sensing, BLE streaming, Android labelled field recording, PC researcher-side monitoring, compact local decision logic, and haptic cue delivery. This contribution is treated as a system implementation outcome rather than as a commercial wearable product.

The second contribution is a labelled pilot data-collection workflow for static sitting and instructed pelvic rotation in everyday seated contexts. This workflow supports a person-independent classifier evaluation and separates the detection task from broader claims about daily-life activity recognition.

The third contribution is a sensor-comparability evaluation between the custom XIAO-based wearable and a research-grade Shimmer IMU. The comparison is used to assess trend-level signal agreement for the studied movement task, not to claim clinical-grade pelvic-angle validity.

The fourth contribution is an embedded feasibility assessment of a compact KNN detector on the target microcontroller. This includes timing and memory measurements for the deployed firmware path and a haptic reminder trigger, while remaining outside the scope of battery-life validation and behavioural effectiveness testing.

\section{Thesis Outline}

Chapter~\ref{ch:related_work} reviews related work in a monitor-to-intervention progression: real-world prolonged-posture monitoring, real-time intervention for prolonged sitting, and closed-loop monitor-to-feedback systems. Chapter~\ref{ch:methods} describes the methods in objective order. It first presents the prototype hardware and software technical route, then the labelled sitting dataset and classifier evaluation, then the Shimmer/XIAO comparability protocol, and finally the embedded deployment timing and memory protocol. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports the corresponding results for prototype implementation, pelvic-rotation detection, sensor agreement, and embedded feasibility. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the engineering trade-offs, detection boundary, sensor-validity limitations, and future work. Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} concludes the thesis by summarising what each objective demonstrates and where the evidence remains limited.
